# TurboQuant KV-cache quantization in suiban

suiban's headline memory feature: the KV cache of every model slot runs with a
quantized V cache using **TurboQuant** scalar quantization, `TQ4_0` (4.5 bits
per value) by default, `TQ3_0` (3.5 bpw) in the aggressive preset, implemented
as new GGML types in our vendored patchset against the PrismML llama.cpp fork
(`suiban/vendor/`, pinned tag `prism-b9596-9fcaed7`).

## The algorithm (and where we deviate from the paper)

TurboQuant (Zandieh, Daliri, Hagemann, Voinea, Han,
[arXiv:2504.19874](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.19874)) is an online vector
quantizer with near-optimal distortion rate. Its core observation: after a
random rotation, the coordinates of KV vectors are approximately i.i.d.
Gaussian, so a fixed per-coordinate Lloyd-Max codebook designed for N(0,1) is
near-optimal: no calibration, no data-dependent state, O(d log d) per vector.

What we implement, per 32-value block:

1. **RMS scale**: compute the block RMS, store as fp16 `d`, normalize the
   block to unit RMS. (All-zero blocks store `d = 0` and round-trip to exact
   zeros.)
2. **Randomized Hadamard transform**: deterministic per-index sign flips from
   the golden-ratio hash (`sign[i] = ((i * 0x9E3779B9) >> 31) ? −1 : +1`),
   then a 5-stage Walsh-Hadamard butterfly, normalized by 1/√32. The transform
   is involutive, so decode uses the same butterfly.
3. **Lloyd-Max lookup**: each rotated coordinate is mapped to the nearest
   centroid of an optimal N(0,1) scalar codebook: 8 levels (3-bit codes) for
   TQ3_0, 16 levels (4-bit) for TQ4_0. Boundaries are centroid midpoints.

**Honesty about the rotation**: the paper never says "Hadamard". Its rotation
is a QR-decomposed Gaussian random matrix. The RHT above is the
community-validated substitution (used by the MIT-licensed reference port we
build on, and by the consensus in the llama.cpp community discussion
[ggml-org/llama.cpp#20969](https://github.com/ggml-org/llama.cpp/discussions/20969)):
it preserves
the Gaussianization property that the codebook needs while being O(d log d)
with zero stored state. We measured the resulting distortion against the
paper's targets (table below). The substitution holds.

**MSE variant only, no QJL**: the paper also describes a QJL-based inner-product
sketch for keys. Community consensus (and our adopted rule) is to drop QJL
entirely: attention scores go through a softmax, which amplifies small errors
in K far more than in V, and the QJL estimator's variance on exactly the
high-score keys that matter is the wrong trade. suiban therefore uses **no QJL
anywhere**, and K stays at `q8_0` (see below).

## Block formats

| type | GGML id* | layout | bytes / 32 values | bpw |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `TQ3_0` | 43 | fp16 RMS scale + 12 B of 3-bit codes | 14 | 3.5 |
| `TQ4_0` | 44 | fp16 RMS scale + 16 B of 4-bit codes | 18 | 4.5 |

*IDs are internal to our patched fork build (appended after the fork's
`Q2_0 = 42`); they are not mainline GGML ids. Block size 32 divides every
Bonsai head dimension (128 and 256), and keeps flash-attention tile
parallelism intact.

The TQ3_0 codebook is ported verbatim from the MIT reference
(`{±0.2451, ±0.7560, ±1.3439, ±2.1519}`); the TQ4_0 codebook is generated by
`vendor/codebooks/generate.py` (stdlib Lloyd-Max on N(0,1), converged < 1e-12,
cross-checked against Max 1960 to ≤ 2e-4). Theoretical codebook MSEs: 0.034548
(8-level) and 0.009501 (16-level).

## Why K stays q8_0

Quantization error in K perturbs attention *logits*, and the softmax turns
small logit noise into disproportionate probability shifts, especially for
the few high-attention keys that dominate a head's output. Error in V only
perturbs the post-softmax weighted average, which is far more forgiving. So
suiban quantizes aggressively where it is safe (V) and conservatively where it
is not (K = `q8_0`, ~8.5 bpw): the default pair `K=q8_0 / V=TQ4_0` costs
~26 KiB per token on the 27B versus ~64 KiB for f16/f16, while keeping K
distortion negligible. This asymmetric design matches the paper's own quality
reference point: at an average 3.5 bits for the cache, TurboQuant *ties the
full-precision cache* on LongBench (50.06). That number is the quality bar we
cite in the user-facing disclaimer, not a claim we re-measured.

## Presets and the fallback chain

Presets (surfaced in `/v1/settings` → `kv.preset`, state in `/v1/system` →
`kv.turboquant`):

- `recommended`: K=`q8_0`, V=`TQ4_0` (default)
- `aggressive`: K=`q8_0`, V=`TQ3_0`
- `off` (disclaimer toggle unchecked): K=`q8_0`, V=`q8_0`

Degradation is **never a crash and never silent**: every step down emits a
`notice` (SSE event + `/v1/system.notices`):

```
V=TQ4_0/TQ3_0
  └─ TurboQuant kernels missing (e.g. prebuilt binary) or FA off → K=q8_0, V=q8_0
       └─ flash attention unavailable at all                     → K=f16,  V=f16
```

(Quantized V hard-requires flash attention in llama.cpp; without FA the only
safe cache is f16.) Default installs run prebuilt fork binaries **without**
TurboQuant (`backend_supported=false`, `fallback_active=true` + notice);
`suiban install turboquant` clones, patches and builds the fork locally
(CPU/CUDA backends only) and swaps the binary.

## Measured end-to-end results (GPU, 2026-07-21)

One machine, honestly labeled: RTX 3070 Ti Laptop 8 GB, backend CUDA, fork tag
`prism-b9596-9fcaed7` + this patchset (0001–0007), model Bonsai 27B **1-bit**
(`Q1_0`), K=`q8_0`. Full `suiban bench kv` battery: `llama-perplexity` runs
as a subprocess of the bench (6 × 2048-token chunks of a deterministic
shuffled-word corpus: unpredictable by construction, so the absolute PPL is
huge and meaningless; the **deltas** are the signal), plus needle retrieval
at 10/50/90 % depths of 4k/8k/16k-token haystacks and a canned ~30-turn
agentic replay (6 early-fact probes answered live):

| V cache | PPL (llama-perplexity) | vs q8_0 | needle 4k/8k/16k | replay |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `q8_0` (baseline) | 1362.08 ± 67.8 | n/a | 3/3 · 3/3 · 3/3 | 6/6 |
| **`tq4_0` (default)** | 1363.54 ± 67.8 | +0.11 % (inside the error bar) | 3/3 · 3/3 · 3/3 | 6/6 |
| `tq3_0` (aggressive) | 1361.15 ± 67.4 | −0.07 % (inside the error bar) | 3/3 · 3/3 · 3/3 | 6/6 |
| `q4_0` | 1361.04 ± 67.8 | −0.08 % (inside the error bar) | 3/3 · 3/3 · 3/3 | 6/6 |

All four configurations are statistically indistinguishable at this sample
size: TurboQuant V-cache is **measured quality-neutral here**, consistent with
the earlier integration-pass run (PPL deltas inside error bars on a different
synthetic corpus), the paper's LongBench result (3.5-bit ties full cache at
50.06) and community reports. A synthetic battery cannot prove the absence of
a real-workload regression: it can only fail to find one; re-measure on your
own machine with `suiban bench kv`
(report: `~/.bonsai/reports/bench-kv-<date>.md`). Decode/prefill **speed**
including the patch-0007 fast path: [`benchmarks.md`](benchmarks.md).

## Measured kernel results (CPU, this machine, patched fork)

`tests/test-quantize-fns` (all-types run passes, exit 0):

```
tq3_0 absolute quantization error:    ok (0.004182)   [tolerance 0.0060, from the MIT reference]
tq3_0 dot product error:              ok (0.061406)   [tolerance 0.15]
tq4_0 absolute quantization error:    ok (0.002111)   [tolerance 0.0030, set empirically]
tq4_0 dot product error:              ok (0.017466)   [tolerance 0.15]
```

`vendor/run_kernel_tests.py` stage 3 (100 000 random N(0,1) 32-blocks):

```
tq3_0: relative MSE 0.032450  (paper target ≤ 0.0345)   cosine similarity 0.983655
tq4_0: relative MSE 0.008921  (paper target ≤ 0.0096)   cosine similarity 0.995532
```

Stage 4 stresses the white-noise assumption with heavy-tailed (Student-t,
df=3) and per-channel-outlier (×100 spikes on 2 of 32 coordinates) inputs over
10 seeds. Measured, honestly: TOTAL relative MSE does not degrade (worst
0.0314/0.0206 for tq3_0, 0.0086/0.0089 for tq4_0: the RMS scale plus the
rotation keep the rotated coordinates bounded), **but** under channel outliers
the non-outlier channels reconstruct at 4.3–5.0× (tq3_0) / 2.9–3.0× (tq4_0)
their own energy, worse than zeroing them. That is the real
per-channel-outlier cost of any block-RMS format, and the harness asserts it
as a measured band instead of hiding it inside the healthy total. Stage 5
verifies head-dim rows (64/96/128/256, multi-block) round-trip with an
unchanged byte layout.

Stage 6 validates the CUDA kernels' dequant algorithm host-side (the CUDA
fattn path inverts the rotation with a closed-form parity sum instead of the
CPU's butterfly) on every distribution, seed and head dim: max deviation from
the C reference is 5.8e-07/5.2e-07 (white noise) and 3.3e-05/4.7e-05 on
×100-outlier blocks (tolerance scales with the block scale). Stage 7 then runs
the **compiled** kernels on a GPU against the CPU reference: dequantize rows,
`SET_ROWS` KV-write quantize and `FLASH_ATTN_EXT` vec + prefill paths;
measured max deviations for this machine are tabled in
[`../vendor/README.md`](../vendor/README.md).

The ~0.9955 cosine for TQ4 matches theory for white Gaussian input
(√(1−MSE)); the ~0.997 community figure came from real KV tensors, which are
more structured than white noise. Perplexity/needle quality deltas on a real
Bonsai model, measured on real hardware, are in "Measured end-to-end results"
above; re-measure on your own machine with `suiban bench kv`.

## Naming caveat

Mainline GGML's `TQ1_0`/`TQ2_0` (ids 34/35) are **TriLM ternary weight**
formats, completely unrelated to TurboQuant despite the similar names. Our
types are `TQ3_0`/`TQ4_0` at ids 43/44 inside the patched fork only, and are
KV-cache-oriented (usable as tensor types generally, but designed and tested
for the cache path).
